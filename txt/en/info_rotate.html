Aspect of a map

Rotating the globe

As we have seen, a map projection transforms the sphere onto a plane. We can, prior to apply this transformation, rotate the sphere in the space, so all the points, curves, and any object on the sphere is placed in a different position in space. If we apply the projection afterwards, all these objects are placed in a different (distorted) position in the plane than before.


Rotations on the sphere change the aspect.

This way, we can make any point on the globe to appear at the center of our projection. In cartography, this is called the aspect of a map. Maps in equatorial aspect are centered at a point on the equator (usually the intersection of the equator with Greenwich meridian); maps in polar aspect are centered at one of the two poles; and maps in oblique aspect are centered at any other point of the globe.

Some maps are more interesting on one particular aspect. For instance, Mercator map displays loxodromes better in equatorial aspect, or the Azimuthal Equidistant map is better centered at our location.

  • Select the Aspect tool and drag the map to change the aspect. You can also choose the equatorial, polar or oblique aspects on the selector.
